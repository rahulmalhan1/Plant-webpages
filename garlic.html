Garlic

(Allium sativum)

What is Garlic?

Garlic is a species in the onion genus, Allium. Its close relatives include the onion, shallot, leek, and chive. It is a bulbous plant native to Central Asia and northeastern Iran and has been used as both a food flavoring and a traditional medicine for thousands of years. The distinctive, pungent "head" or bulb is the part most commonly used.

Botanical Description

The garlic plant produces a composite bulb, which consists of several individual cloves, each enclosed in a thin, papery sheath. This bulb grows underground. Above ground, the plant produces long, flat, grass-like leaves. It can also send up a tall stem called a "scape," which may produce small flowers or bulbils (small aerial cloves).

Culinary & Medicinal Uses

Garlic is a fundamental ingredient in cuisines worldwide.

Key Uses:

  • Culinary: Used raw or cooked to add a sharp, pungent, and savory flavor to dishes. It forms the base of countless recipes, from pasta sauces to stir-fries.
  • Cardiovascular Health: Extensively studied for its potential to help lower blood pressure and cholesterol levels.
  • Immune Support: Contains allicin, a compound with antimicrobial and antiviral properties, making it a popular folk remedy for colds.
  • Antioxidant: Provides antioxidants that help protect against oxidative stress.

How to Grow Garlic

Garlic is grown from individual cloves, not seeds.

  • Planting: Typically planted in the fall. Each clove is planted pointed-end-up a few inches deep.
  • Sunlight: Requires full sun.
  • Soil: Needs loose, fertile, well-drained soil.
  • Harvesting: Harvested in mid-summer when the lower leaves start to turn brown and dry. The bulbs are then cured (dried) in a shady, airy location.
