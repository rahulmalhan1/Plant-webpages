Aloe Vera

(Aloe barbadensis miller)

What is Aloe Vera?

Aloe Vera is a succulent plant species of the genus Aloe. It is an evergreen perennial, originating from the Arabian Peninsula, but grows wild in tropical, semi-tropical, and arid climates around the world. It is widely cultivated for its medicinal and cosmetic uses. The clear gel found inside its leaves is the most commonly used part.

Botanical Description

Aloe Vera is a stemless or very short-stemmed plant that grows to 60–100 cm (24–39 in) tall, spreading by offsets. The leaves are thick and fleshy, green to grey-green, with some varieties showing white flecks on their upper and lower stem surfaces. The margin of the leaf is serrated and has small white teeth. It produces yellow, tubular flowers on a spike in summer.

Traditional & Modern Uses

The gel from the Aloe Vera leaf is renowned for its healing properties. It is a common household remedy for skin conditions and is also used in many commercial products.

Key Benefits:

  • Skin Care: Famously used to soothe sunburns, minor cuts, and rashes. It acts as a protective layer on the skin and helps replenish moisture.
  • Moisturizer: The gel is an excellent natural moisturizer for the skin and hair.
  • Digestive Health: Aloe Vera juice is sometimes consumed to aid digestion and relieve constipation, though this should be done with caution.
  • Anti-inflammatory: It possesses anti-inflammatory and cooling properties.

How to Grow Aloe Vera

Aloe Vera is a very easy plant to care for, making it a popular houseplant.

  • Sunlight: Prefers bright, indirect sunlight. Direct, hot sun can burn the leaves.
  • Soil: Needs well-draining soil, such as a cactus or succulent mix.
  • Watering: Water deeply but infrequently. Allow the soil to dry out completely between waterings. Overwatering is the most common way to kill an aloe plant.
  • Propagation: Easily propagated from "pups" or offsets that grow from the base of the mother plant.
