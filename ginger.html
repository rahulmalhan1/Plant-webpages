Ginger

(Zingiber officinale)

What is Ginger?

Ginger (Zingiber officinale) is a flowering plant whose rhizome, or underground stem, is widely used as a pungent, aromatic spice and a traditional medicine. It is a herbaceous perennial belonging to the same family as turmeric and cardamom (Zingiberaceae). It is native to Maritime Southeast Asia and is now cultivated globally.

Botanical Description

The ginger plant grows to about 1 meter (3 ft) tall. It produces slender, grass-like leaves from a sheath. The plant also produces a separate, shorter stem with small, pale yellow and purple flowers. The valuable part is the "hand" or rhizome, which grows horizontally underground. It is knobby, branched, and has a beige, papery skin with a pale yellow, fibrous interior.

Culinary & Medicinal Uses

Ginger's spicy, warming flavor is central to many cuisines, and its medicinal properties are prized worldwide. Its primary active compound is gingerol.

Key Uses:

  • Anti-Nausea: Famously used to alleviate nausea and vomiting, especially from motion sickness and morning sickness.
  • Culinary Spice: A staple in Asian cuisine (stir-fries, curries) and also used in baking (gingerbread), beverages (ginger ale, ginger tea), and candies.
  • Anti-inflammatory: Used to help reduce inflammation and pain associated with conditions like osteoarthritis.
  • Digestive Aid: A well-known carminative (gas-relieving) and digestive tonic.

How to Grow Ginger

Ginger is grown from pieces of the rhizome.

  • Climate: Needs a warm, humid, tropical climate. It does not tolerate frost.
  • Sunlight: Prefers partial shade or filtered sun, not direct, harsh sunlight.
  • Soil: Requires very rich, fertile, and well-drained soil.
  • Watering: Keep the soil consistently moist, especially during the growing season.
