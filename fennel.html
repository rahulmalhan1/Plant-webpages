Fennel

(Foeniculum vulgare)

What is Fennel?

Fennel is a hardy, perennial herb in the carrot family (Apiaceae). It is native to the Mediterranean region but is now widely cultivated in many parts of the world. Every part of the fennel plant is edible: the bulb, the stalks, the feathery leaves (fronds), and the seeds. It is known for its distinct, anise-like flavor.

Botanical Description

Fennel can grow to heights of up to 2.5 meters (8 ft). It has hollow, ribbed stems and finely dissected, feathery leaves. The plant produces large, flat clusters (umbels) of small, bright yellow flowers. The "bulb" (known as Florence Fennel or Finocchio) is a swollen, overlapping leaf base that is eaten as a vegetable.

Culinary & Medicinal Uses

Fennel's anise flavor makes it a unique ingredient.

Key Uses:

  • Culinary (Bulb): The crisp bulb is eaten raw in salads, or cooked (braised, roasted, or grilled), which mellows its flavor.
  • Culinary (Fronds): The delicate leaves are used as a fresh herb to garnish dishes.
  • Culinary (Seeds): Fennel seeds are a common spice, used in sausages, bread, and as a primary component in Indian "mukhwas" (a post-meal digestive aid).
  • Digestive Aid: Fennel tea, made from the seeds, is a very popular traditional remedy for indigestion, bloating, and gas.

How to Grow Fennel

Fennel is grown from seed and is a cool-weather crop.

  • Sunlight: Requires full sun.
  • Soil: Prefers rich, moist, and well-drained soil.
  • Planting: Sow seeds directly in the garden in spring or late summer. It has a long taproot and does not transplant well.
  • Bulbing: To encourage a tender bulb to form, mound soil up around the base as it swells (a process called blanching).
