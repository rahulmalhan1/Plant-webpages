Sandalwood

(Santalum album)

What is Sandalwood?

Sandalwood (Santalum album, or Indian Sandalwood) is a class of woods from trees in the genus Santalum. The woods are heavy, yellow, and fine-grained, and unlike many other aromatic woods, they retain their fragrance for decades. Sandalwood oil is extracted from the woods for use. It is one of the most expensive woods in the world.

Botanical Description

Indian Sandalwood is a small, evergreen tree that grows to about 4 to 9 meters (13 to 30 ft) in height. A unique characteristic of the tree is that it is a semi-parasite. Its roots form connections (haustoria) to the roots of other trees to draw water and some nutrients. The valuable, fragrant heartwood, which can take 15-30 years to develop, is found in the center of the trunk.

Uses & Cultural Significance

Sandalwood's distinct, woody, and warm aroma has made it highly valued for centuries across many cultures.

Key Uses:

  • Religious Ceremonies: In Hinduism and Buddhism, sandalwood paste is used for anointing deities, in sacred rituals, and for creating a "tilaka" on the forehead.
  • Perfumery: Sandalwood oil is a prized ingredient in high-end perfumes, where it serves as a base note to fix other fragrances.
  • Aromatherapy: The oil is widely used in aromatherapy for its calming and grounding effects, helping to reduce anxiety and promote relaxation.
  • Skincare: Sandalwood paste and powder are used in traditional face masks to soothe skin, reduce blemishes, and provide a cooling effect.
  • Carving: The wood is used for intricate carvings, especially for religious idols and ornate boxes.

Cultivation

Growing sandalwood is a long-term and complex process, primarily undertaken in large-scale commercial plantations rather than home gardens.

  • Parasitic Nature: It must be grown with suitable host plants (like neem or acacia) from which it can draw nutrients.
  • Time: It takes many years for the tree to mature and develop its valuable heartwood.
  • Protection: Due to its high value, the trees are vulnerable to poaching and are often grown in protected areas.
