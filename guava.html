Guava

(Psidium guajava)

What is Guava?

Guava is a common tropical fruit cultivated in many tropical and subtropical regions. It is the fruit of a small tree (Psidium guajava) in the myrtle family (Myrtaceae). The fruit is prized for its intense, aromatic fragrance and sweet flavor.

Botanical Description

The guava tree is a small, branching tree or shrub that typically grows to 3–10 meters (10–33 ft) tall. It has distinctive smooth, mottled, copper-colored bark that peels off in flakes. The leaves are oblong, leathery, and aromatic when crushed. The fruit is a berry, round or oval, with a green to yellow skin. The flesh inside can be white, yellow, or a deep pink, and contains many small, hard seeds.

Culinary & Medicinal Uses

Guava is enjoyed both as a fresh fruit and in processed forms.

Key Uses:

  • Fresh Fruit: Eaten fresh, often with a sprinkle of salt and chili powder in some cultures.
  • Processed Foods: The fruit is very high in pectin, making it perfect for making jams, jellies, and "guava paste" or "guava cheese." It is also very popular as a nectar or juice.
  • High in Vitamin C: Guava is a nutritional powerhouse, containing 4-5 times more Vitamin C than an orange.
  • Medicinal (Leaves): Guava leaf tea is a traditional remedy in many cultures for diarrhea and digestive ailments.

How to Grow Guava

Guava trees are hardy and productive.

  • Climate: Needs a warm, tropical or subtropical climate. It is sensitive to frost, especially when young.
  • Sunlight: Requires full sun to produce abundant fruit.
  • Soil: Adapts to a wide range of soils but prefers well-drained soil.
  • Propagation: Can be grown from seed, but named varieties are propagated by cuttings or air-layering to ensure fruit quality.
