Moringa

(Moringa oleifera)

What is Moringa?

Moringa (Moringa oleifera) is a fast-growing, drought-resistant tree native to the Indian subcontinent. It is often called the "Drumstick Tree" (for its long, slender seed pods), the "Miracle Tree" (for its dense nutritional value), or the "Horseradish Tree" (for the taste of its roots). Nearly every part of the plant is edible and used for food or medicine.

Botanical Description

Moringa is a slender tree with drooping branches. It has a corky, greyish-white bark. The leaves are feathery and compound, made up of many small, oval leaflets. It produces fragrant, yellowish-white flowers. Its most notable feature is the fruit: long, slender, three-sided pods (the "drumsticks") that hang down and contain dark, winged seeds.

Nutritional & Medicinal Uses

Moringa is considered a "superfood" due to its incredible nutritional profile.

Key Uses:

  • Nutritional Powerhouse: The leaves are exceptionally rich in vitamins (like A and C), minerals (calcium, potassium), and protein.
  • Culinary (Leaves): The leaves are cooked like spinach or dried and powdered to be used as a nutritional supplement.
  • Culinary (Pods): The immature seed pods ("drumsticks") are a common vegetable in South Asian cuisine, used in curries and soups (like sambar).
  • Water Purification: The seeds can be crushed and used to purify water, causing sediment and bacteria to clump and settle.

How to Grow Moringa

Moringa is very easy to grow in the right climate.

  • Climate: Thrives in hot, dry, tropical, and subtropical climates. It is not frost-tolerant.
  • Sunlight: Loves full, direct sun.
  • Soil: Prefers well-drained, sandy soil. It is highly drought-tolerant.
  • Growth: Grows extremely fast and can be cut back hard (coppiced) to encourage bushy leaf production.
